The Problem

Designing a Zero-Friction Repurchase Flow That Turns Waste Data Into Smarter Buying Decisions

WasteFree collects valet trash at apartment communities, and their trash trucks have the technology to sort, recycle, and scan every item disposed by the residents. WasteFree has the data - but residents have no way to see it, act on it, or benefit from it. There was no tool that gave users waste insights and also gave them the choice to make actionable interactions.

The challenge: design an app that shows residents what they threw away and gives them a frictionless path to buy it again — or buy a smarter alternative. The app had to work for residents of all tech levels, from digital natives to people who rarely use apps.

Research & Discovery

Dozens of Screens, One Core Question: What Does the Resident Actually Need?

WasteFree delivered a comprehensive specification outlining two full product experiences—one for residents and one for managers/owners—spanning dozens of screens along with an extensive list of future features. My first priority was to identify what was essential to the resident experience and define a clear path for what could be deferred to later phases.

Discovery: What I Learned from the Client

  • The repurchase flow is 90% of expected app activity — WasteFree was explicit about this being the core
  • Residents choose a preference during onboarding (Save Money / Be Healthier / Save the Planet) that shapes all product recommendations
  • The app is free for residents — revenue comes from apartment management subscriptions and data monetization to CPG brands (~$35/unit/month)
  • Leasing agents introduce the app during move-in, significantly reducing in-app onboarding burden
  • The WasteFree Score is like a FICO score — formula hidden, criteria shared, format TBD
  • Push notification is the primary entry point to the app

Competitive Analysis

Analyzed 13 apps across 4 categories (waste tracking, sustainability, smart repurchase, gamification) to identify UX patterns, gaps, and opportunities for WasteFree.

Key Finding: No existing app bridges "what you throw away" to "what you should buy instead." Most waste and sustainability apps either educate or inform without driving behavior change.

User Personas

Four Residents, one app - designed for them all

Each persona aligns to a core motivation—saving money, improving personal health, or reducing environmental impact—which directly informs their preferred journey through the app. These motivation paths serve as the foundation for the experience, shaping feature prioritization, content hierarchy, and overall interaction design.

  • Outcome: 4 core resident personas representing key user segments
  • Key Insight: Each persona is driven by a primary motivation: Save Money, Be Healthier, Help the Environment
  • UX Strategy: These motivations define distinct user paths that guide feature prioritization, content hierarchy, and user flows across the app
  • Impact: Ensures the experience is tailored, relevant, and scalable across different user types without overcomplicating the product

Flows & Interactive Prototypes

Three Flows That Define the Entire Resident Experience

Click through each prototype to experience the flow. Flow diagrams show the user journey logic above each interactive wireframe.

Flow 1 — Onboarding

Leasing agent introduces app
User opens app
Creates account + resident info
Quick survey (grocery, delivery, Prime)
Chooses global preference (Save Money / Be Healthier / Save the Planet)
Customizes per category (optional)
Grants notification permission

WasteFree onboarding happens before the user (resident) ever sees WasteFree — leasing agents explain why WasteFree matters during move-in and offer the WasteFree service to tenants as a complimentary service for being a resident at the property. The app collects resident info, a quick survey about their shopping habits (to power fulfillment options), and one critical preference that shapes every product recommendation. Per-category preference refinement (e.g., "Save Money on groceries, Be Healthier on cosmetics") is introduced later in-app through progressive disclosure — keeping onboarding fast while still capturing the data WasteFree needs. Target: under 60 seconds from open to done.

This is 90% of the app's expected activity. A resident gets a push notification after their trash is collected, taps it, and lands directly on their item list. Each item offers three actions — buy the same thing again, buy a WasteFree alternative based on their onboarding preference, or add to cart for later. The "Buy it WasteFree" path is where the preference from onboarding (Flow 1) comes to life — the alternative product changes based on whether the resident chose Save Money, Be Healthier, or Save the Planet. Use the preference toggle in the prototype to see how the experience shifts.

Approach A — Smart Default: The checkout pre-selects a single fulfillment provider based on the resident's onboarding profile (e.g., Amazon Prime). A "From your profile" badge builds trust, and an expandable "Change" dropdown keeps the UI clean for the majority of users who won't need to switch. This is the fastest path to checkout and the recommended approach for V1.

Approach B — Per-Item Fulfillment: Same flow as above, but at checkout each cart item gets its own fulfillment selector (Amazon, Instacart, DoorDash, HEB). This gives residents maximum flexibility but adds cognitive load. A yellow warning tells residents items from different stores may arrive separately. Compare the complexity with Approach A above — navigate both to the Checkout screen (step 7) to feel the difference.

Flow 3 — Impact & Score

User taps "My Impact" tab
Sees WasteFree Score
Scrolls to community ranking
Sees waste breakdown by material
Taps category → sees recycling method
Views tangible impact
Shares or toggles date range

The WasteFree Score works like a FICO score for waste. A sticky date toggle at the top (Last Pickup / This Week / This Month / YTD) controls all four sections at once. Community ranking is the primary engagement hook — residents see how their building compares. The waste-stream breakdown was revised based on WasteFree's feedback to use real material designations (PET, HDPE, OCC, Ferrous, Non-Ferrous, Glass, Organic) with friendly names for resident comprehension. "Non-recyclable" was renamed to "Landfilled" for transparency, and "Composted" was added as its own stream. Tapping any category reveals how that material was recycled (Closed-Loop, Downcycled, Remelted, Composted) with a plain-English explanation — progressive disclosure keeps the default view clean while giving depth on demand. A glossary link explains all recycling terms.

Flow 4 — Collection & Service Data

Resident taps "Collection" tab
Sees bag metadata + all collected items
Taps item → sees detail + recycling method
Can help identify unknown items (3-field form)

Originally scoped as low priority, WasteFree requested this flow be added after reviewing the Phase 2 wireframes. This is the raw data transparency layer — while Flow 2 (Repurchase) only shows identified products the resident can rebuy, Flow 4 shows everything WasteFree collected, including unidentified trash with photos. The lead screen now combines collection history with the item summary: residents first see bag-level metadata (number of bags, collection time, collector name/employee ID, violation status) before scrolling into individual items. Each item displays its waste-stream designation (PET, OCC, Organic, etc.) and how it was processed. Unidentified items get a photo badge — tapping reveals the sorting photo and a simplified 3-field identification form (brand, product category, recycling category) so residents can crowdsource item recognition. This flow builds trust through radical transparency: residents see exactly what happened to every item in their bag.
